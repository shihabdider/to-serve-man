---
title: Alignment
author: Shihab Dider
---

Alignment refers to the coincidence of the following:
1. What you think you want
2. What the agent thinks you want
3. What you actually want

When the three are in agreement, alignment is achieved. When even one is misaligned, the work is doomed to failure. Luckily, it is possible to set things up so that you attain alignment quickly and reliably.

## What you think you want
You don't know what you want. It's a mistake to assume otherwise and it's useful to retain a healthy skepticism and loose grip on such platitudes as objectives, goals, and projects, at least when working with agents. The flip side of this is the Cambrian explosion of possibility, the so called "yak-shaving" catalyzed by agents (which might be another flavor of AI psychosis). When you can make anything, there's a drive to make everything, when most of the time you were better off making nothing at all.

The future cheats us from afar; better to focus on present pains. Have the agent give you a Mom Test (or any other such "customer interview" like interaction) to tease out the vital from the merely salutary. Such excerise will, at the very least, prevent your fall into the vast echo chambers that are not at all discouraged by agentic sycophancy. Play the devil's advocate and the steelman (make sure to clear your context when you do this). Keep an open mind.

## What the agent thinks you want
Misalignment from agent hallucination or divergence is overblown. Providers are being incentivized (with hundred of billions of dollars) to address this problem, and in many ways the "intelligence" of a model, the "inference" it does, is a measure of how well it can follow and execute human instructions. 

Agents are now quite capable, so what we have here is a failure to communicate. It is a failure to understand the limitations and biases of human and LLM cognition (such as they are). It is a failure present information through using interfaces that accomodates those limitations. Better to always assume that the agent misunderstood you and have it frequently paraphrase its understanding than take it at its word. Verify, then trust.

## What you actually want
The fastest way (because it is the only way) to figure out what you actually want is through the process of elimination; the credo of Michelangelo, to chisel away the superfluous. This suggests a way of working in fast iterations. But bear in mind that as model capabilities increase, and because it will always be faster to verify than to implement, it will increasingly be more efficient to increase the amount of work per unit.
