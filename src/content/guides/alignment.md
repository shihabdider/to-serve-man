---
title: Alignment
author: Shihab Dider
---

Alignment refers to the coincidence of the following:

1. What you think you want
2. What the agent thinks you want
3. What you actually want

When all three are in agreement, alignment is achieved. When even one is misaligned, the work is doomed to failure. Luckily, it is possible to set things up so that you attain alignment quickly and reliably.

## What you think you want
You don't know what you want. It's a mistake to assume otherwise, and it's useful to retain a healthy skepticism and a loose grip on such platitudes as objectives, goals, and projects, at least when working with agents. The flip side of this is the Cambrian explosion of possibility, the so-called "yak-shaving" catalyzed by agents (which might be another flavor of AI psychosis). When you can make anything, there's a drive to make everything, even though most of the time you would be better off making nothing.

The future cheats us from afar; it is better to focus on present pains. Have the agent give you a Mom Test (or another such customer-interview-style interaction) to tease out the vital from the merely salutary. Such an exercise will, at the very least, prevent your fall into the vast echo chambers of agentic sycophancy. Play devil's advocate and steelman the opposing position (make sure to clear your context when you do this). Keep an open mind.

## What the agent thinks you want
Misalignment from agent hallucination or divergence is overblown. Providers are being incentivized (with hundreds of billions of dollars) toward a solution, and in many ways the "intelligence" of a model—the "inference" it does—is a measure of how well it can follow and execute human instructions.

Agents are now quite capable, so what we have here is a failure to communicate. It is a failure to understand the limitations and biases of human and LLM cognition (such as they are). It is a failure to use interfaces that accommodate those limitations.

Always assume that the agent has misunderstood you, and have it frequently paraphrase its understanding. Verify, then trust.

## What you actually want
The fastest way (because it is the only way) to figure out what you actually want is through the process of elimination, following the credo of Michelangelo: chisel away the superfluous. This seems to suggest working in fast iterations. But as model capabilities increase, and because it will always be faster to verify than to implement, it will become increasingly more efficient to let the agent implement more and more of the work before you verify—to let it veer wildly off course and then demand a harsh correction, thereby increasing the learning rate, so to speak.
